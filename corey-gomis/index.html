- The 3 Systems -
Slides 8-9 // System One

Pricing That Actually Builds Profit

Being busy doesn't guarantee profitability.

When I looked at my own numbers early on, I realized I was pricing the way most contractors do: based on what competitors were charging and what felt right. Not based on what the job actually cost me to deliver. It usually comes down to underpricing, limited cost visibility, or inefficiencies.

Pricing should include labor, overhead, and profit, not just what competitors charge. It starts with a detailed budget that defines your daily revenue targets and ensures every job is priced for profit.

What I built:

  • A detailed operating budget that told me exactly what I needed to bill per day, per crew, to stay profitable
  • Job costing that tracked actual vs. estimated on every project
  • A pricing structure that accounted for overhead, materials, labor burden, and profit margin
  • Thoughtful price adjustments that strengthened margins without damaging trust
Slides 10-12 // System Two

Building a Business That Runs Without You

The real metric isn't revenue. It's owner-independent revenue.

Many contractors find themselves stuck working in their business instead of building one that truly works for them. When you add more jobs and more crews but every new job still requires your involvement, growth actually makes the problem worse.

Three shifts I made to step away:

  • Shift 1: Hiring a real manager. Not just a lead hand. Someone who can make decisions, not just follow instructions.
  • Shift 2: Getting pricing out of my head. I documented my estimating logic so someone else could do it at 90% of my accuracy.
  • Shift 3: Managing by numbers, not by feel. Simple dashboards showing margins, completion rates, and crew productivity in real time.

Before the Shifts

First one on the job, last one to leave. Every estimate through me. Phone rang constantly. Revenue growing, hours growing faster. Good money, zero freedom.

After the Shifts

Team handled day-to-day operations. Estimating system others could run. Problems resolved without my input. Evenings and weekends back. Real freedom and control.

Slides 13-16 // System Three

Making Your Business Worth Buying

The best time to start planning your exit is years before you intend to sell.

Most trade owners wait too long to prepare their company for sale and end up losing value or getting stuck running a business they no longer love. Buyers look for more than revenue. They look at how the business runs, how consistent it is, and how much it depends on the owner.

What buyers actually evaluate:

  • Recurring and repeat revenue - predictable revenue is worth more than one-off project revenue
  • Customer concentration risk - no single client should represent more than 15% of revenue
  • Key-person dependency - if the value walks out the door when the owner does, there's nothing to buy
  • Documented processes - can someone else follow your systems?
  • Clean financials - messy books make buyers question everything

Sells at 1.5x - 2x (or doesn't sell)

Owner runs everything. Pricing in the owner's head. No documented processes. Books are a mess. No systems a buyer could step into.

Sells at 3x - 4x+ (with leverage)

Manager runs day-to-day. Pricing system anyone can follow. SOPs for key processes. Clean financials. Business is a transferable asset.

The 3-year exit runway: Year 1 reduces owner dependency. Year 2 diversifies and systematizes. Year 3 optimizes and positions for sale.
