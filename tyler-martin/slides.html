1

Gross Profit Margin by Service Line

  • Not your overall margin. Per service line. They are different.
  • The job you think makes the most money might be the one killing your margins
  • Healthy margins in trades typically range from 40-60%, depending on the service
  • If you don't know this number, you can't price correctly and you can't decide what to sell more of
TALK: "Number one is gross profit margin, but not the overall number. I need you to know this per service line. Most contractors I work with are surprised when we break it down. They have one service line doing 55% margin and another doing 22%. And the 22% one is usually the one they're pushing hardest because it generates the most revenue. But revenue without margin is just activity. You need to know which services actually make you money."
ThinkTyler
06
2

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

  • What it actually costs to get a new paying customer through the door
  • Most owners have never calculated this because they've never tracked it
  • Ad spend + your time on estimates + referral costs + close rate = real CAC
  • If CAC is higher than first-job profit, you're paying to work
TALK: "Number two is your customer acquisition cost. This is the total cost to get a new paying customer. And I mean total. Ad spend, the time you spend driving to estimates, referral fees, the labor cost of your sales process, all divided by your close rate. Most owners have never run this math. When they do, the number is almost always 2-3x higher than they assumed. If your CAC is higher than your first-job profit, you're literally paying money to go do work."
ThinkTyler
07
3

Breakeven Point

  • The monthly revenue number where you stop losing money
  • Below this number, every month costs you regardless of how busy you are
  • Most owners are surprised at how high it actually is once overhead is included
  • Changes how you think about monthly targets and seasonal planning
TALK: "Number three is your breakeven point. This is the monthly revenue where you stop losing money. Not where you start making good money. Where you stop losing it. You take your fixed costs, rent, insurance, truck payments, salaries, divide by your gross margin percentage, and that's the number. Most owners I work with are surprised at how high it is. And once you know it, you stop guessing about monthly targets. You have a hard floor. Everything above it is real profit. Everything below it is a problem."
ThinkTyler
08
4

Cash Conversion Cycle

  • The time gap between spending money on a job and collecting payment for it
  • In trades, this gap is commonly 30 to 90 days
  • The number one reason profitable businesses run out of cash
  • Growth actually makes this worse if you don't manage it
TALK: "Number four is the cash conversion cycle. This is the gap between when you spend money, materials, labor, fuel, and when you actually collect payment. In trades, that gap can be 30, 60, even 90 days. And here's the problem: the faster you grow, the worse this gets. You're buying more materials, hiring more people, running more jobs, and the cash from those jobs doesn't show up for weeks or months. This is the number one reason profitable businesses run out of cash. They're profitable on paper but broke in the bank account."
ThinkTyler

5

Owner's Discretionary Earnings

  • The number a buyer looks at when valuing your business
  • Net profit + your salary + perks + one-time expenses = ODE
  • This is what determined the value of my engineering firm at exit
  • Most owners have never calculated it, even if they want to sell someday
TALK: "Number five is owner's discretionary earnings. This is the number that determines what your business is worth if you ever want to sell it. It's your net profit, plus your salary, plus any personal expenses you run through the business, plus one-time costs that wouldn't carry over to a buyer. This is the number that determined what my engineering firm was worth at exit. Multiply ODE by a market multiple, typically 2-4x for trades businesses, and that's your valuation. Most owners have never calculated it. Which means they're building a business without knowing what it's actually worth."

Plumbing Company: CAC Was 3x What They Thought

A plumbing company was tracking their ad spend and assumed their customer acquisition cost was around $180. When they included the owner's time driving to estimates, the close rate on those estimates, and the admin time per lead, the real number was $540.

On their average job size, $540 CAC meant they were barely breaking even on first-time customers. Their entire profit came from repeat business they weren't systematically generating.

TALK: "A plumbing company thought their CAC was $180 because that's what they were spending on ads per new customer. But when we added in the owner's time driving to estimates, the close rate on those estimates, and admin time processing leads, the real number was $540. On their average job, that meant they were barely breaking even on first-time customers. All their profit was coming from repeat business, and they had no system to generate repeat business. They were relying on customers calling back on their own. Once they saw the real CAC, they changed their pricing, improved their close rate process, and built a retention system."
