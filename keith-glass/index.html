PART 2

The 4 Numbers That Tell You Everything

The 4 NumbersSection header
Slide 9 of 25
Number 1

Daily Job Count: "The Magic Number"

How many jobs did you run today? Not this week. Not this month. Today.

Most plumbing company owners cannot answer that question at 5 PM on a Tuesday. And if you don't know your daily job count, you have no idea whether your company made money today or lost money today.

Your magic number is the minimum number of daily jobs you need to cover overhead, pay your techs, and still put profit in your pocket. Every plumbing company has one, and almost none of them know what it is.

If you're running 6 trucks and you don't know your magic number, you're guessing. And guessing is how plumbing companies go broke.

The 4 NumbersIntroduces the Daily Tracking Report concept.
Slide 10 of 25
Number 2

Close Rate: The 93% Threshold

What percentage of the calls you go on turn into paying jobs? If you don't know, you're not tracking it. If you're not tracking it, your techs know they can cherry-pick the easy calls and leave money on the table.

Here's a number most plumbing coaches won't give you: 93%. When your close rate hits 93%, that's not a celebration. That's a signal that you're too cheap. If homeowners are saying yes to almost everything, your prices are too low.

A healthy close rate for a properly priced plumbing company sits between 75% and 85%. If you're above 90%, raise your prices. You're leaving money on every single call.

The 4 NumbersCounterintuitive insight. High close rate = underpricing.
Slide 11 of 25
Number 3

Average Ticket

What's your average invoice? And more importantly, do you know what it should be?

Most plumbers look at their average ticket and compare it to what they hear other guys charging. That's the wrong comparison. Your average ticket needs to reflect your overhead, your labor costs, your truck costs, and a real profit margin. Not what some plumber in another state charges for a water heater.

When I sit down with a new coaching client and we actually calculate what their average ticket should be based on their real numbers, it's almost always higher than what they're charging. Sometimes significantly higher.

Are you charging what you are worth? Or are you charging what you think the market will bear?

Number 4

Truck Inventory ROI: $800 vs. $5,000

I've climbed into hundreds of plumbing trucks over the years. Some guys have $5,000 worth of parts on the truck and can't finish half their calls without a supply house run. Other guys carry $800 of the right parts and close out 90% of their calls on the first visit.

The difference isn't how much you spend on inventory. It's whether you're carrying the parts your customers actually need based on your call history. If you're stocking fittings you use once a quarter and missing the ones you need three times a week, your truck inventory is costing you money instead of making you money.

$800 of the right inventory beats $5,000 of the wrong inventory every single day. Track what you use, stock what you need, stop guessing.

Are You Charging What You're Worth?

PricingSection header
Slide 16 of 25

You're Not Charging for Your Hands. You're Charging for What You Know.

There's an old story about a plumber who shows up, looks at the pipes for two minutes, taps one fitting with a wrench, and the problem is fixed. The homeowner says, "You're charging me $200 for two minutes of work?" The plumber says, "I'm charging you $10 for the tap and $190 for knowing where to tap."

That story is funny, but here's why it matters: most plumbing company owners are still pricing like they're charging for the tap. They price based on time and materials when they should be pricing based on the value of the solution they're delivering.

CustomerOptions™ Pricing gives homeowners choices and gives your company the margins it deserves. It's flat rate done right, updated quarterly, and built specifically for plumbing.

PricingClassic teaching story, then introduces proprietary pricing system.
Slide 17 of 25

"If You Don't Ask, You Will Not Receive!"

I tell every plumber I coach the same thing: you will never get a price you don't ask for. Homeowners are not going to volunteer to pay you more. But when you present options, explain the value of each one, and let them choose? They pick the higher option more often than you'd expect.

The biggest pricing mistake in plumbing isn't overcharging. It's under-offering. You show up with one option and one price, the customer says yes or no, and you leave. When you present three options with clear differences in value, the average ticket goes up without a single argument about price.

Your price books should be updated quarterly. Not annually, not when you remember, quarterly. Material costs change, labor rates change, and if your prices don't change with them, your margins are shrinking while you're not paying attention.
